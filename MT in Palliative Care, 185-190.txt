Music therapy in palliative care

Chapter: Music therapy in palliative care
Author: Clare O’Callaghan

Music is intricately woven into the fabrics of life and takes on deeper significance during times of transition, loss, and grief.

Introduction to music therapy in palliative care

Music therapists offer musical experiences to improve comfort and enhance the lives of palliative care patients and their families. Music’s helpful role in dealing with transition and loss is long evident in tribal rituals and religious and community practices (Laderman and Roseman, 1996). In the 1970s, pioneering music therapists Lucanne Magill and Susan Munro respectively brought live music to patients at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, New York, and The Royal Victoria Hospital Palliative Care Unit, Montreal. Music therapists now contribute to holistic patient care in palliative inpatient and home-based services, and other settings caring for people with degenerative conditions, throughout the world.

In palliative care, music therapy can be defined as the creative and professionally informed use of music in a therapeutic relationship with people identified as needing physical, psychosocial, or spiritual help, or desiring further self-awareness, to enable increased life satisfaction and quality. Music therapists, who are university trained and accredited by national registration committees, can often extend the way that music enhances patients’ well-being and their connection with who and what matters in their lives. The musical elements and evolving therapeutic relationship can underlie helpful, sometimes transformative experiences. The focus is on therapeutic process rather than musical products and participants do not have to have musical backgrounds to benefit. Music therapists invite patients and families to explore and choose music therapy methods. The therapeutic relationship informs how the music is shared and created. It can be distinguished from music thanatology, which is the provision of prescriptive music using harp and voice at bedside with the compassionate musician’s presence. This chapter describes ways in which music therapists work in palliative care, clarifies research supporting its efficacy, and offers strategies for how caregivers may offer music to support palliative care patients when music therapists are not available.

Music therapy in adult contexts: assessment, methods, and effects

Various descriptions of music therapy in palliative care with adult patients, families, and staff carers are available. Music therapists typically receive referrals from staff, patients themselves, or their families. As patients’ conditions fluctuate, sessions may be flexibly scheduled, range from minutes to over one hour, and be offered occasionally to almost daily. Therapists can visit patients at bedsides in hospitals and homes bringing accompanying instruments, sheet music, or recorded music. Music therapy groups may also be conducted in day hospices, inpatient palliative care, or nursing home settings. Many music therapy departments incorporate extensive recorded music libraries, audio equipment, and tuned and untuned instruments.

Upon meeting patients, the music therapist’s assessment includes determining the patient’s music preferences, the relevance of music throughout their lives, and biopsychosocial needs or spiritual and aesthetic interests that may be addressed through music therapy. Music therapy methods offered to patients and their families include replaying the music of their lives, including live performance, music listening, music and life review, and lyric substitution in familiar songs; exploring new music, including therapeutic songwriting, music improvisation, and unfamiliar music; guided use of music such as relaxation inductions with live or recorded music; and music-based gift or legacy creation, such as song compositions and music-based audio-visual recordings.

Some music therapy aims in palliative care include supportive validation of one’s feelings and thoughts, of a life that has been and is still being well lived, and of one’s self-worth and spiritual way of being; opportunities for contemplation; increased self-awareness to aid coping; symptom relief and relaxation including pain, tension, dyspnoea, nausea, insomnia, and restlessness; connectedness for those with cognitive impairment or language barriers and expanded opportunities for interactions with family members, friends, other patients, and staff; aesthetic and spiritual experiences including pleasure, diversion, normalcy, creative expression, and transcendence; and support for expression of grief and bereavement.

Replaying the music from one’s life

Patients often choose to experience music that elicits emotions, messages, or memories of places, events, and people they want to feel connected with. Identifications with lyrics associated with adversity, loss, and hope, and with singers who have lived with life-threatening conditions, can enable one to feel understood and part of a wider human experience. Music is polysemous and can be interpreted in multiple ways, nurturing or sustaining in unexpected ways. Music can also reconnect patients with non-patient identities, elicit memories, laughter, and tears, and foster dignity-conserving perspectives and continuity of self. Music-based reminiscence can improve communication, validate lives, enhance insight, affirm cultural identity, and improve self-esteem and sense of worth. Life stories and favourite musical pieces can be audio-recorded as legacies, especially meaningful for patients unable to verbally communicate.

Physical reactions to music therapy may reveal vital aspects of personhood and dignity-conserving care. Music therapy experiences can affirm life roles, community contributions, and extend self-awareness and expression. Song lyric substitution can be particularly powerful, as music gives impetus to personal expression and hearing one’s words mirrored back through song can soothe, affirm, and inspire pride. Music can also access non-verbal awareness and foster relaxation or peacefulness, and therapists carefully balance music, discussion, and counselling to support patients’ needs.

Patients and families

Music therapy can create intimate contexts for patients and families to convey supportive messages through shared music, memories, touch, singing, and movement. It can sustain family members during bedside vigils and support communication even when patients are minimally responsive. Shared musical experiences can ease bereavement distress and inspire shared involvement in multi-bed settings. Combining music with allied therapies such as physiotherapy or art therapy can broaden therapeutic benefits and support identity, empowerment, and spiritual connection.

Exploring new music

Therapeutic songwriting

Through individual or group songwriting, patients and families can express important sentiments in contained and efficient ways. Songwriting can foster pride, spiritual comfort, self-affirmation, cathartic relief, relationship closure, and life review. Structured support enables participation even for those with cognitive impairment. Song lyrics written by palliative care patients commonly express messages, memories, imagery, prayers, reflections on adversity, and connections with significant others. Songwriting for children can support parent–child connectedness and help children cope with bereavement.

Improvisation

In music therapy improvisation, therapists and participants improvise together on tuned or untuned instruments, with or without vocalization. Pentatonic scales are often used to ensure harmonic coherence. Improvisation allows clients to express creative aspects of self, be affirmed through musical mirroring, and experience enjoyment, freedom, and transformation. Musical dialogue can inspire further creativity and adaptive self-awareness.

Concerts and music appreciation

Music therapists may organize concerts and music appreciation sessions in response to patients’ interests, offering novel, educative, and enjoyable experiences. Performers may include patients, families, staff, or community musicians.

Guided use of music

Relaxation inductions and music can help manage symptoms and reduce stress, including during medical procedures. Preferred music is most associated with relaxation and pain reduction. Music may alter mood and pain perception through physiological, cognitive, and emotional mechanisms. Therapists often match and gradually shift musical elements to support desired states. When patients control their music choices, adverse effects are rare.

Specific populations

Children and adolescents

Music plays a central role in young people’s lives and can offer normalcy, emotional expression, coping support, and identity development. Music therapy can alleviate distress, improve mood, reduce anxiety during procedures, and support family bonding, even near end of life.

Cognitive impairment

Musical memory and neural systems are often preserved in cognitive impairment. Combining music and language can enhance connection, expression, and aesthetic experience. Music therapy can also support patients in low-awareness states and contribute to revised diagnoses and meaningful family engagement.

Ethnic minorities

Culturally significant music can reduce isolation, affirm identity, assist expression of pain and grief, and support preparation for death and family grieving.

Bereavement

Pre-loss music therapy supports healthy grieving by creating positive shared memories. Music therapy groups and songwriting can help bereaved adolescents and children express emotions, accept loss, and maintain continuing bonds.

Music-based care and the relevance of music therapists

Caregivers are encouraged to invite patients and families to use music for self-care whenever possible. Music therapists are recommended to extend music’s capacity to enhance life quality through therapeutic relationships and evidence-based methods.

Therapeutic relationship in music therapy

The music therapist’s presence can function as a sounding board or human mirror, reflecting patients musically, verbally, and non-verbally. Live musical involvement can validate experiences, support coping, and foster awareness. As patients approach death, music therapy may focus on comfort, continuity, and supporting family expression rather than psychotherapeutic change.

Research and evidence

Extensive research supports music therapy in palliative care, including Cochrane reviews in end-of-life care, oncology, and dementia. Findings indicate improvements in quality of life, mood, relaxation, spiritual well-being, comfort, and reductions in pain, anxiety, distress, and depression. Qualitative research highlights music therapy as a meaningful social, emotional, and spiritual experience. While randomized controlled trials face methodological challenges, both quantitative and qualitative studies contribute valuable insights into music therapy’s benefits.

Closure

Music therapists invite patients and caregivers to explore musical attachments and creative experiences to alleviate distress, sustain identity, validate contribution, and enable connection, realization, and joy. Music can carry mourning beyond words, provide a holding space, and support release. Music therapists are vital members of palliative care teams supporting people and those accompanying them at the end of life.
