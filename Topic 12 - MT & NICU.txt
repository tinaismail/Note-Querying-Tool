WHAT IS NICU?

Neonatal Intensive Care Unit (NICU), specializes in the care of ill or premature newborn infants. Neonatal refers to the first 28 days of life.

A premature baby is born before 37 completed weeks of gestation (more than 3 weeks before the due date).

BACKGROUND

• Music therapy in NICUs began over 25 years ago
• Initially faced resistance from medical staff
• Now well-established with over 300 specially trained Board Certified Music Therapists in the U.S.
• Over 50 research studies in refereed journals provide evidence-based methodology for NICU-MT and document important and unique infant benefits from music.
• Evolving in international settings (There are over 50 NICUs in Ontario, about 3 have a music therapy program).

TRAINING TO WORK AS A MT IN NICU?

• National Institute for Infant and Child Medical Music Therapy certification
• Three-phase training program for Board Certified Music Therapists
• International participation in specialized training
• Knowledge of fetal development, medical standards, and infant responses

PREMATURE INFANTS: PERSPECTIVES ON NICU-MT PRACTICE
By Jayne M. Standley

FAMILY CENTRED CARE

• In 2004, a survey of NICU nurses in the U.S. demonstrated that 68% wanted music included in NICU treatment
• Individualized developmental and family-centered care
• Just as important to support the parent(s)?

CAN MUSIC BE DANGEROUS?

Problems occur when music is used in the NICU setting without expertise.

Volunteer musicians seek opportunities to perform in the NICU without knowing the consequences of overstimulation or how to use music to the greatest benefit.

Parents or other professionals sometimes bring toys or mobiles into the NICU environment with auditory components such as lullabies or heartbeats without understanding the consequences for their own or nearby infants.

PRIMARY CRITICAL VARIABLES MEASURED

Physiologic measures
(heart rate, respiration rate, weight gain, oxygen saturation, days in hospital, apnea, bradycardia, etc.)

Developmental/behavioral milestones
(behavior state, ability to transition from state to state or to self-soothe, feeding skill, and caregiver/infant attachment).

In order to clarify benefits of specific procedures, the music used should be fully described with careful attention to content, complexity, duration, and decibel (dB) level

(Standley & Walworth, 2010).

LIVE OR RECORDED MUSIC?

• Research shows that music is effective in multiple ways whether live or recorded.
• Live music is the preference of the profession, there are too many premature infants for their needs to be fully met by the limited number of music therapists available to provide services.

3 STAGES OF DEVELOPMENTAL CARE OF PREMATURE INFANTS

    Survival/Pacification Stage (23-30 weeks)

    Cautious Stimulation Phase (30-34 weeks)

    Interactive/Discharge Phase (34-40 weeks)

(Westrup, 2007; Standley & Walworth, 2010).

STAGE 1: SURVIVAL/PACIFICATION

• No touching, no interacting, no disturbance of sleep
• Uninterrupted music listening with minimal stimulation
• Quiet, consistent music (<65 dB Scale C)
• Benefits: soothes, masks aversive stimuli, enhances oxygen saturation

STAGE 2: CAUTIOUS STIMULATION

• Limited touch permitted (unmoving hand on chest)
• Infant should remain in relaxed state
• Music during passive touch/kangaroo care
• Gender differences in response to stimulation

STAGE 3: INTERACTIVE/DISCHARGE

• Establishing sleep/wake cycles
• Developing suck/swallow/breathe coordination
• Learning to tolerate and respond to stimuli
• Orienting to and tracking stimuli
• Music supports all these developmental milestones

IN CONCLUSION

• Neurological hypersensitivity: Music therapy promotes homeostasis
• Failure to thrive: Music therapy improves physiological wellbeing
• Apnea: Music with gentle touch supports neurological maturation
• Feeding difficulties: Music therapy enhances non-nutritive sucking
• Attachment issues: Music therapy strengthens parent-infant bonds

NICU MUSIC THERAPY: SONG OF KIN AS CRITICAL LULLABY IN RESEARCH & PRACTICE

Joanne Loewy – DA, LCAT, MT-BC
Director of Louis Armstrong Center for Music & Medicine at Mount Sinai Health System.

INTRODUCTION

• Live music therapy improves neonatal vital signs in NICU
• Data from 272 infants across 11 NICUs shows effectiveness
• "Song of kin" (parent-selected music) enhances infant-parent bonding
• Benefits: improved weight gain, better sleep patterns, prolonged quiet-alert states

THE NICU

• Machinery and noise can threaten infant-parent bonding
• Dependence on healthcare professionals may undermine natural attunement
• Parents of premature infants experience high stress levels
• Music therapy creates space for interactive nurturance

METHODOLOGY

• 272 infants with respiratory distress, sepsis, and small gestational age
• 17 certified music therapists and 8 graduate interns (trained for replication)
• Live Singing (10 mins) - interventions randomized over 2-week period (Twinkle/song of kin) – or No music.
• Measurements: heart rate, respiratory rate, O₂ saturation, activity level (pre/during/post)
• Parental stress assessed before and after intervention

WHAT IS ENTRAINMENT?

• Synchronization of physiological rhythm by external stimulus
• Allows purposeful influence on the body's ability to regulate
• Live music therapy provides direct means of entrainment

PRECOMPOSED VS SONGS OF KIN

• Parents identified culturally relevant songs
• "Twinkle, Twinkle Little Star" used as default lullaby
• Parents encouraged to sing as first option
• Familiar voices provide comfort and stability
• Singing entrained to match infant's breathing rhythm

KEY FINDINGS: WITHIN-SESSION CHANGES

• Increase in quiet-alert time during lullaby intervention
• Lower heart rates recorded during lullaby
• Greater response to lullaby than control condition

KEY FINDINGS: LONG-TERM OUTCOMES

• Marked increase in percentage of sleep time over 2 weeks
• Different effects based on lullaby type:

    "Twinkle" showed higher oxygen saturation levels

    Song of kin showed higher sucking rates and calorie intake
    • Significant decrease in parental stress levels

BENEFITS OF SONG OF KIN

• Enhances feeding behaviors:

    Higher sucking rates

    Increased calorie intake
    • Represents cultural heritage and family preferences
    • Provides continuity of care from NICU to home
    • Strengthens infant-parent bonding

CONCLUSION

• Live music therapy improves vital signs and sleep patterns
• Parent-selected songs enhance bonding and reduce parental stress
• Music therapy provides continuity of care from NICU to home
• Further research needed on specific music elements and long-term outcomes

SUMMARY

• Music therapy can positively impact health care goals in NICU
• MT training is important
• Both live and pre-recorded music can be implemented (pending specific goal, one may be more appropriate than another).
