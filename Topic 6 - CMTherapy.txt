Even Ruud
Community Music Therapy

A whole new discourse labeled “community music therapy” is gradually evolving in the field of music therapy. Community music therapy is a way of doing and thinking about music therapy where the larger cultural, institutional, and social context is taken into consideration. The approach involves an awareness of the systems music therapists are working within. It means that music therapy is not only directed toward the individual, but is often aimed at changing the system that is sometimes part of the client’s situation.

Researching the history of music therapy reveals that this idea is not entirely new. In many countries there has been a tradition of therapists working within community mental health systems, especially from the 1970s onward in the United States and many European countries. In Great Britain, there has also been a tradition among musicians to take their art back to the community and give performances as a form of social service. This has been labeled “community music” (Ansdell 2002).

As Stige (2003:124) remarks, this idea may not be new at all. Examination of musical healing traditions in indigenous cultures reveals that entire communities are often involved in healing rituals (Gouk 2000).

Some music therapists may therefore look for what is new in this development and see only its links to traditional music therapy practice. Others may notice how this community-oriented approach is changing not only the goals, vocabulary, and language of music therapy, but also the practice itself. An approach to music in therapy that is sensitive to culture and context speaks of solidarity and social change. It tells stories of music as identity building, empowerment, and agency. Community music therapy addresses the humanization of communities and institutions and is concerned with health promotion and mutual care.

Definitions

When Ken Bruscia, in Defining Music Therapy (1998), outlined areas of music therapy practice, he included “ecological practices.” Bruscia described the primary focus as “promoting health within and between various layers of the socio-cultural community and/or physical environment” (Bruscia 1998:229). He further explained:

“This includes all work which focuses on the family, workplace, community, society, culture, or physical environment, either because the health of the ecological unit itself is at risk and therefore in need of intervention, or because the unit in some way causes or contributes to the health problems of its members. Also included are any efforts to form, build, or sustain communities through music therapy. Thus, this area of practice expands the notion of ‘client’ to include a community, environment, ecological context, or individual whose health problem is ecological in nature. Helping an individual to become healthier is not viewed as separate from improving the health of the ecological context in which the individual lives. Conversely, helping an ecological context to become healthier is not separate from improving the health of its members. Helping individual and ecology to relate harmoniously makes both healthier.”

Bruscia emphasized the influence of systems theory in this area of practice. During the twentieth century, influenced by information and communication theory, it became clear that phenomena are interrelated. Systems theory offered an alternative to linear cause-and-effect models, proposing a circular understanding of interactions. It was influenced by cybernetics, concerned with regulation and feedback in systems. Key figures included Norbert Wiener and Ludwig von Bertalanffy. Bertalanffy formulated the principle that the whole is greater than the sum of its parts. Seeing with two eyes, for example, provides depth and distance perception beyond what one eye alone offers (Kenny 1989).

The traditional way

When music therapy was reinvented as a modern profession in the mid-twentieth century, it became affiliated with institutions and dominant ideologies. It was incorporated into university programs, and research developed within a natural science paradigm. Music therapy was constructed as a treatment profession emphasizing the individual relationship between therapist and client. Therapy took place within medical or special education frameworks, and music functioned as a means to regulate the therapeutic relationship.

For many years, music therapy paid little attention to larger social forces or cultural contexts. Boundaries were drawn between music therapy and music education, community music practices, or alternative healing traditions. Therapy typically occurred inside institutions, often isolated from the outside world. Even families or peers were sometimes excluded. The biomedical model of illness limited consideration of social conditions, networks, and cultural contexts. Systemic thinking was not yet well developed within music therapy.

A “new music therapy”

Gradually, music therapists came to understand ill health and disability as embedded in social systems and material processes. Illness may arise not only from physical causes, but from disempowerment, ignorance, and lack of social understanding. Music was recognized as a unique tool for involving others, empowering marginalized individuals, and restoring access to symbolic and expressive resources. Music therapy began to bridge individuals and communities, creating spaces for shared musicking and human values.

Music therapists increasingly work with entire communities, focusing on systemic interventions. Music builds networks, offers symbolic resources to underprivileged individuals, and empowers subordinated groups. Music has again become a social resource for healing communities as well as individuals. Music therapists may become educators in health-related musicking, teaching people to care for their health through music. Musicking may be understood as “immunogen behavior,” a health-performing practice, echoing ancient traditions such as those of Pythagoras.

Three examples from Norway

To illustrate community-oriented music therapy, three Norwegian examples are presented. Music therapy in Norway, since the 1970s, has emphasized cultural issues in defining health, illness, therapy, and music itself (Ruud 1990).

Music therapy was defined as the use of music to give people new possibilities for action. Illness was understood as not only biological but also social. Practice was built upon prevailing musical codes, using culturally resonant music such as amateur music, rock bands, and children’s music.

The first example, from Stige (2002, 2003), shows how therapists involve the wider community. In the 1980s, Stige initiated projects integrating people with mental disabilities into society. He observed their exclusion from communal musical life. Alongside developing their musical skills, he worked to change attitudes within local music groups. Short-term performance projects involving all participants helped break down barriers. Local brass band music played a central role.

The second example involves Venja Ruud Nilsen’s work with female inmates. Weekly band rehearsals led to a rock band culture within the prison. Many participants became skilled musicians. Continued participation after release provided support crucial to avoiding drugs and re-incarceration.

The third example comes from Trygve Aasgaard’s research in pediatric hospital settings. Through song creation with children with severe illnesses, such as cancer, Aasgaard showed how songs affected relationships with staff, families, and friends. Arranging songs for hospital staff choirs allowed new forms of interaction. Children were seen not only as patients but as composers expressing their experiences artistically.

These examples show music therapy engaging broader social and institutional contexts. Therapists worked with culturally familiar musical forms and involved others in the clients’ systems, including staff, families, and communities. Music therapy extended beyond the therapy room to build connections across systems.

Cultural issues in music therapy

Ecological practices and community music therapy challenge traditional definitions of music therapy. They take culture seriously in shaping therapeutic needs and practices, developing new roles and identities. These ideas energize socially engaged music therapists.

For those entering the field in the 1970s, music therapy was seen as a potential agent of social change (Ruud 1988). It was viewed as a way of life and a social movement, not just a treatment profession. Today, music therapists increasingly cross boundaries between therapy and community music-making, reclaiming music’s original cultural functions.

Music ethnography shows that music exists in all cultures and has played roles in healing, education, cosmology, and social organization. In modern society, music continues to regulate emotions, identities, and social life. Research highlights everyday musicking as a resource for wellbeing and coping with contemporary life (DeNora 2000; Juslin and Sloboda 2001).

From the eighteenth century onward, music became separated from everyday life through aesthetic ideologies emphasizing autonomous art. This led to elitism and separation from practical purposes. In the postmodern climate, differentiation gives way to integration. Music increasingly intersects with economics, marketing, and medicine, and popular forms gain legitimacy.

Music is widely used for identity formation, relaxation, stress management, pain relief, and sleep regulation. People use personal soundscapes to manage mood and attention in daily life (Bull 2000).

Music as a cultural immunogen

Music may be understood as a technology for achieving improved health. Using the concept of “affordance,” music allows certain uses and interpretations based on its phenomenological profile (DeNora 2000; Sloboda and Juslin 2001). Music evokes emotion through associations and structural expectations, and this impact can be harnessed for health promotion.

Sociological studies show music regulating mood, attention, and energy across contexts. Musicking mediates the relationship between individuals and situations, aligning psychological states with environmental demands.

Music can be viewed as a behavioral or cultural immunogen, comparable to health-promoting behaviors such as exercise or sleep hygiene. Community music therapy thus has a responsibility to highlight music’s role in health planning and promotion, alongside warnings about harmful sound exposure.

Health and quality of life

Health is often defined biomedically as the absence of disease. Many people, however, understand health as positive wellbeing and surplus energy. This perspective aligns health with quality of life, a concept shaped by values from different professions. Music contributes to quality of life by providing vitality, empowerment, social connection, and meaning (Ruud 1998).

A future music therapy

Music therapy may be ready to move from the margins to a central societal role. Aligning with broader music practices, it can revitalize music’s healing and self-regulatory functions and reclaim music as a humanizing force in everyday life.
