Chapter 4 – Music Therapy Interventions

DEFINITIONS

Music therapist
An individual who has met all the mandates of their regulating professional association to provide music therapy services.

Patient or Client?
An individual receiving music therapy services could be considered a patient or a client. An individual who is hospitalized or receiving music therapy as part of a health care program is often referred to as a patient, whereas an individual who is receiving music therapy through a private practice is usually referred to as the client.

INTRODUCTION

No two music therapy sessions are alike. The length of the session, types of treatment goals, speed of goal progression, and combination of techniques used depend entirely on the client’s needs and preferences. A patient treatment plan is designed to best meet the needs of the patient while acknowledging the strengths and training of the music therapist. Although the methods are chosen from a collection of established interventions common to the music therapy profession, the music therapist is free to creatively implement different music therapy interventions, tailoring the technique to best address the patient’s goals.

Kenneth Bruscia, a prominent American music therapist and educator, has published numerous impactful articles and books on the practice of music therapy. In his book Defining Music Therapy, Bruscia describes that “music affords the client opportunities to develop multifaceted relationships within and between the self, others, and the various worlds in which they live. In music, these relationships can be manifested and experienced physically, musically, mentally, behaviorally, socially, or spiritually” (2014, p. 41). In addition to exploring the diverse nature of music’s effects on overall physical and mental health, he also stresses the importance of the individualization of treatment and the careful selection of both the techniques and the music itself.

Bruscia believes that music therapy relies on the effects of receiving or producing the sounds and vibrations of music, and as such, he classifies music therapy interventions as either receptive or active. Receptive interventions involve listening to music in order to relate to concepts in the song, access memories, or experience the soothing effects of meaningfully chosen music on both the body and the mind. During these interventions, the patient is a passive recipient of the music.

In contrast, active interventions focus on cognitive, creative, vocal, or instrumental participation in music, most often a combination of these. During these interventions, the patient is an active participant in the music. This allows for free expression and communication in a judgment-free environment. While some people benefit most from either receptive or active interventions, a combination of both is also common.

For example, an individual with limited cognitive and physical abilities as a result of a progressed medical condition may not be able to engage in active music therapy interventions but could benefit from listening to meaningful music. The beat of the music can help regulate heart rate and stabilize breathing. The melody and instrumentation can induce relaxation and elevate mood. Receptive engagement can also act as a distraction from pain perception.

Active music therapy interventions can provide the same benefits, with the addition of fostering a sense of autonomy and offering tools for physical rehabilitation and self-expression. For example, an individual who has experienced a stroke may be working toward speech-related goals and improving gait, as well as psychological goals related to post-stroke lifestyle losses such as loss of job, mobility, or relationships. Active interventions may include walking to music with a strong predictable beat, singing familiar meaningful songs, songwriting, and improvising.

This chapter discusses six of the most common music therapy interventions: facilitated music listening (receptive), and the active interventions of using pre-composed music, singing, improvisation, lyric analysis, and songwriting. These techniques are only considered music therapy when implemented by a credentialed music therapist.

OVERVIEW OF INTERVENTIONS

Pre-composed music: memory, expression, emotion, and communication
Facilitated music listening: psychological and physiological effects of music
Singing: emotional expression, socialization, respiratory and cognitive function, speech disorders
Improvisation: free expression of emotions, non-verbal communication
Lyric analysis: expression and processing of thoughts, feelings, and emotions
Songwriting: creative expression and communication

PRE-COMPOSED MUSIC

The influence of music is dependent on extrinsic factors such as memories and associations, and intrinsic musical elements such as rhythm, melody, and harmony (Clark & Tamplin, 2016). Certain songs evoke strong memories and emotions, allowing individuals to vividly re-experience moments from their lives.

Music has powerful effects on the brain. Songs become associated with people, places, events, and emotions. While this can result in positive recollections, music can also evoke traumatic memories. When used carefully, pre-composed music can be a powerful therapeutic tool for individuals experiencing memory impairment, stress disorders, mood disorders, dissociation, or cognitive decline.

In music therapy, pre-composed music is used to help patients recall memories, experience positive emotions, or therapeutically explore difficult memories. Musical memories often remain intact even when other memories fade, making music an important grounding tool.

Pre-composed music can be delivered live or recorded, individually or in groups. Recorded music may evoke memories through specific qualities of the original performance, while live music allows the therapist to adapt tempo, emphasis, and structure in real time.

Group sessions using pre-composed music can foster connection, shared understanding, and reduced loneliness among individuals with common experiences such as grief, trauma, military service, or incarceration. Individual sessions allow for highly personalized music selection.

Patient engagement may range from passive listening to active participation such as tapping, singing, or movement. Active participation can improve motor coordination and motivation, especially when familiar music is used. Pre-composed music can also strengthen family connections through shared playlists and collaborative song selection.

FACILITATED MUSIC LISTENING

Facilitated music listening uses carefully selected music to promote receptive benefits on the body and mind. Music may be live or recorded, familiar or unfamiliar, pre-composed or improvised. Familiar music is often most effective.

This intervention is commonly used for anxiety reduction, pain management, and emotional regulation. Live music allows the therapist to adjust tempo, rhythm, and volume in response to the patient’s physiological cues such as breathing or heart rate.

Music entrainment occurs when bodily rhythms align with musical rhythm, influencing heart rate and breathing. Facilitated listening can reduce stress hormones, increase endorphins, dopamine, and serotonin, and improve immune function and brain activity.

Psychological benefits include distraction from stress, reduced anxiety, improved mood, and increased orientation for individuals with cognitive impairment. Music can also distract from pain perception by competing with nociceptive signals in the nervous system.

Facilitated music listening is valuable before, during, and after medical procedures, and may reduce reliance on pain medication or anesthesia. It can also support cognitive engagement and promote movement in individuals with neurological or movement disorders.

SINGING

Singing is an active intervention used to support emotional expression, socialization, respiratory function, speech, cognition, and self-confidence. No prior singing experience is required.

Singing can be individualized in one-on-one sessions or used in groups to foster community and connection. Group singing may provide opportunities for performance and shared accomplishment.

Singing benefits mental health by providing emotional release, improving self-esteem, and encouraging motivation. It is especially helpful for children and adolescents.

Physiologically, singing strengthens respiratory muscles, increases lung capacity, and supports motor coordination. It is also effective for speech disorders, as singing engages different neural pathways than speaking.

IMPROVISATION

Improvisation involves spontaneous music creation and free expression without rules or expectations. It encourages creativity, risk-taking, and emotional exploration.

Improvisation is particularly useful for individuals who struggle with verbal expression due to trauma, developmental differences, or psychological conditions. It supports emotional catharsis and non-verbal communication.

Improvisation can also support physical rehabilitation by encouraging movement, coordination, and strength.

To reduce intimidation, therapists may introduce playing rules such as limiting notes to a pentatonic scale. These guidelines promote confidence and can be gradually relaxed.

Common improvisation techniques include mirroring, matching, grounding, holding, and dialoguing. These techniques foster validation, stability, connection, and communication.

LYRIC ANALYSIS

Lyric analysis involves exploring song lyrics to process thoughts, feelings, and emotions. Lyrics can help individuals articulate experiences, feel understood, and gain perspective.

This intervention is particularly useful for individuals with mental health or substance use disorders, those coping with trauma or loss, and adolescents and young adults.

Lyric analysis may involve verbal discussion, creative interpretation, or non-verbal expression such as drawing. Song selection is individualized and collaborative.

SONGWRITING

Songwriting allows patients to express emotions through original music and provides a tangible product that can be kept or shared. It supports reflection, emotional processing, identity, and self-acceptance.

The music therapist supports brainstorming, structuring, composing, and recording. No prior musical experience is required.

Songwriting techniques include brainstorming, rhyming, fill-in-the-blanks, song parody, and song collage. Songwriting styles vary widely, and rap songwriting has shown particular effectiveness with adolescents.

Example: Songwriting with Mr. Jones

Mr. Jones, an elderly man in long-term care, initially refused therapy. Through poetry written in Greek, he collaborated with a music therapist to create a song. The process restored purpose, motivation, and emotional connection with his family. The song later became part of his legacy and was played at his funeral.

This case highlights that the therapeutic value lies in the process, not just the final product, and demonstrates how songwriting can foster engagement, emotional healing, and connection.

CONCLUSION

Music therapy interventions are highly individualized and adaptable. While this flexibility challenges standardization in research, continued publication of research and case studies is essential. The following chapters explore these interventions across specific illnesses and populations.
