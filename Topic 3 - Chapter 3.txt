**Chapter 3 – Music Therapy in Canada**

Post the introduction of modern medicine, music was rarely witnessed within the context of healthcare. The few practitioners who were using music to support health and wellness goals did not come together until the 1970s, more than 20 years after the profession of music therapy had already been established in the USA and the UK. The field faced significant challenges throughout its development and, as a result, educational programs for music therapy only started to become available in Canada in the mid-1970s; prior to this, those seeking training were required to travel to other countries. Today, music therapy in Canada is supported by various organizations and educational facilities, and music therapists are able to work in a wide range of health care settings. Research in music therapy continues to grow at a rapid rate, providing more acceptance within the health care community as well as opportunities for funding for the development of music therapy programs and further research.

### History of Development of the Music Therapy Profession in Canada

As early as the 1840s, music was being used as a form of treatment in Canadian psychiatric facilities. One article from 1849 described the therapeutic benefits of music and dance at the Beauport Asylum in Quebec, noting the recovery of patients suffering from melancholy and depression. Furthermore, in 1879, Roch Lyonnais—a musician, composer, and music educator from Quebec—traveled to the United States where he presented *The Influence of Music*, a lecture promoting music as a form of therapy.

The occasional and informal use of music in the treatment of psychiatric illnesses in Canada continued into the 20th century. However, it remained an unrecognized field until the influence of American and British advances, in conjunction with the work of Canadian music therapy pioneers such as Norma Sharpe, Alfred Rosé, Fran Herman, and Thérèse Pageau. In the 1950s, while music therapists in the USA and the UK had access to educational programs and professional career development, Canadian music therapists were few and far between and practiced independently due to the lack of provincial or national organizations. This presented challenges in communication, standardization, training, and the development and attainment of accreditation.

Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, seven notable music therapy treatment programs were developed in hospitals and other health care facilities in Canada, despite the absence of educational and training programs, accreditation, or associations. Musician and composer Alfred Rosé developed music therapy programs in 1952 at Westminster Hospital in London, Ontario, and in 1956 at the London Psychiatric Hospital, making these two of Canada’s first music therapy treatment programs. Rosé’s early influence on the field significantly contributed to the development of the music therapy profession.

Also in 1956, music therapist Fran Herman initiated *The Wheelchair Players*, a program that explored the use of music, among other arts, in the treatment of physically impaired children at The Home for Incurable Children, now called Holland Bloorview Kids Rehabilitation Hospital, in Toronto, Ontario. The children participating in this program engaged in various musical activities and performed shows for each other and for the public. Herman’s program inspired many other music therapists to study the use of music therapy not only for psychiatric conditions, but also for physical illnesses.

Two additional programs were developed in the 1950s by Norma Sharpe at the St. Thomas Psychiatric Hospital in Ontario, and by Thérèse Pageau at Hôpital Saint-Jean-de-Dieu in Montreal, Quebec. This was followed by the development of two more programs in the 1960s in Weyburn, Saskatchewan, and Kitchener, Ontario, by Dorothy Twente Sommer and Earl Charboneau, respectively. Despite the success of these programs, music therapy had yet to be recognized as a profession in Canada.

In addition to Norma Sharpe’s program in St. Thomas, her work and accomplishments greatly impacted Canada-wide collaboration and awareness that ultimately led the field to professional success. In 1960, Sharpe conducted a study that reviewed the prevalence of music use in Canadian hospitals and found that many hospitals were in fact interested in, or already practicing, the therapeutic use of music. However, while many of the health care professionals involved had a musical background of some sort, very few were music therapists. Sharpe recognized that, in order for music therapy to become a more commonly practiced form of treatment, collaboration among Canada’s few music therapists was necessary. As such, she began reaching out to fellow music therapists and seeking methods of unification until the 1970s, when she initiated the next major step for the field.

In 1974, Norma Sharpe organized Canada’s first music therapy conference in St. Thomas, Ontario. Although only 63 people attended the conference, it was a success; music therapists across Canada finally had the opportunity to share and exchange experiences, research, interventions, and techniques. The conference was held again over the following two years, growing in popularity each year and leading to two major advances in 1976:

1. The development of Canada’s first music therapy educational program at Capilano University in Vancouver, British Columbia.
2. The founding of the Canadian Music Therapy Association, later renamed the Canadian Association for Music Therapy, and renamed again to its current name, the Canadian Association of Music Therapists.

### Canadian Association of Music Therapists (CAMT)

The formation of the Canadian Association of Music Therapists (CAMT) in 1976 marked the official recognition of music therapy as a profession in Canada. With a vision of “supporting the health and well-being of Canadians through music therapy,” the CAMT is a non-profit organization that aims to support and promote the field of music therapy by providing ethics guidelines and standards of practice; working to expand the scope of music therapy; developing and improving educational programs and field opportunities; serving as a platform for the sharing of information, research, and techniques; and certifying qualified professionals with the designation Music Therapist Accredited (MTA).

By 1979, the CAMT had certified 17 MTAs across Canada, a number that more than quadrupled over the following decade. Pioneers Sharpe, Herman, and Pageau, among others, each served as CAMT presidents throughout the early years of the organization. They were also awarded lifetime membership in the CAMT in recognition of their contributions to the development of the profession. By 2018, the CAMT had expanded to over a thousand members and has since continued to grow.

The CAMT developed the *Canadian Journal of Music Therapy* (CJMT), a peer-reviewed publication that focuses on the sharing of research, case studies, and techniques. This journal brings further credibility to the field by continually producing evidence of the efficacy of music therapy in the treatment of a wide range of health goals. An annual CAMT conference provides further opportunities for professional development, collaboration among music therapists, and the promotion of awareness of the field.

Since the development of the national organization, provincial associations have also formed to provide support and resources at the provincial level. Given that health care is provincially governed in Canada, having both provincial and national associations is necessary to best support music therapists and the profession.

March has been designated as Music Therapy Awareness Month in Canada. This initiative provides opportunities for medical professionals and the general public to learn about the applications and benefits of music therapy. It also helps address challenges faced by the field, including limited public awareness and difficulties in securing adequate funding for research and clinical practice. The need for increased funding and access to services led to the development of the Canadian Music Therapy Fund.

### Canadian Music Therapy Fund (CMTF)

Although research has demonstrated the efficacy of music therapy techniques in treating numerous health conditions, music therapy in Canada is not typically covered by insurance companies, limiting access for those unable to afford services. Founded in 1993, the Canadian Music Therapy Fund (CMTF) is a charitable foundation with the goal of developing and promoting access to music therapy services across Canada. The CMTF works in conjunction with the Music Therapy Centre in Toronto, Ontario, to secure the financial and practical resources necessary to make music therapy accessible to those who may benefit.

The CMTF promotes music therapy services in both urban and remote communities, develops affordable treatment programs for individuals with various diagnoses, and provides music therapists with clinical funding, research grants, and educational scholarships. At Toronto’s Music Therapy Centre, the CMTF also provides subsidized music therapy sessions in both group and individual formats.

---

### Professional Development

Music therapists are required to engage in ongoing professional development in order to maintain their credentialed status through the CAMT. The CAMT and provincial professional associations provide annual conferences offering professional development opportunities. In 2016, the Music Therapy Academy was launched by Rachael Finnerty, MTA and Registered Psychotherapist, to further expand professional development opportunities specific to music therapy. Although based in Canada, the Music Therapy Academy provides workshops and webinars to the global music therapy community and welcomes other health care professionals within their respective scopes of practice.

### Becoming an MTA in Canada

The process of becoming certified as a Music Therapist Accredited (MTA) in Canada involves:

* Post-secondary education
* An internship
* An examination
* Application for certification and CAMT membership
* Continuing education throughout one’s career

Candidates must complete a Bachelor’s or Master’s degree in music therapy (BMT or MMT) at a CAMT-recognized university. The first music therapy degree program in Canada began in 1976 at Capilano University in Vancouver, British Columbia. Today, programs are offered at Wilfrid Laurier University, the University of Toronto, Acadia University, Concordia University, Canadian Mennonite University, and Capilano University.

University programs emphasize a multidisciplinary curriculum that includes music, psychology, music therapy research, anatomy, physiology, biology, counselling, social sciences, and field training. In addition to academic requirements, aspiring music therapists must demonstrate strong musical and therapeutic skills, clinical objectivity, and personal attributes such as reliability, patience, and empathy.

Graduates must then complete a 1,000-hour supervised clinical internship, often incorporated into their degree program. Upon completion, candidates write the Certification Board of Music Therapists (CBMT) examination. Successful candidates may then apply for MTA certification through the CAMT.

To remain in good standing, MTAs must adhere to CAMT ethics guidelines and complete regular continuing education. In provinces where psychotherapy or counselling is regulated, music therapists who incorporate these practices must obtain additional certification from the relevant regulatory body.

### The Clinical Process in Canada

The music therapy process in Canada includes:

* Referral
* Assessment
* Goals
* Treatment plan
* Evaluation and reassessment

Clients may self-refer or be referred by health care or educational professionals. Referrals must be based on health-related goals rather than diagnoses alone. Assessment evaluates cognitive, psychological, and physical well-being and may involve input from family members and health care teams.

Based on assessment findings, the music therapist collaborates with the client to establish goals and implement a treatment plan. Progress is continuously evaluated, and reassessments are conducted as needed. Session length and treatment duration vary based on individual needs, progress, and circumstances.

Music therapy may benefit individuals with a wide range of diagnoses, including dementia, autism spectrum disorder, Parkinson’s disease, mental illness, substance use disorders, chronic pain, acquired brain injury, and cancer. Music therapists work in diverse settings such as hospitals, clinics, schools, prisons, psychiatric facilities, and private practices.

---

### Conclusion

Despite a delayed development compared to the USA and UK, music therapy in Canada is now a regulated, supported, and expanding profession. Following the pioneering work of early leaders, the establishment of the CAMT and the development of educational programs have strengthened the field. Ongoing regulation, research, and professional collaboration continue to enhance the credibility and accessibility of music therapy across Canada.
