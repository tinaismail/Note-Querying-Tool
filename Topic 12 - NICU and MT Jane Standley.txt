Premature Infants: Perspectives on NICU-MT Practice
Jayne M. Standley

Abstract
Music research began in the Neonatal Intensive Care Unit (NICU) over 25 years ago. Initially, medical staff resisted the idea that music therapy could impact premature infant medical outcomes. Today Neonatal Intensive Care Unit–Music Therapy (NICU-MT) is well known in the U.S., with over 300 specially trained Board Certified Music Therapists (MT-BCs), and it is evolving in international settings. Over 50 research studies in refereed journals provide evidence-based methodology for NICU-MT and document important and unique infant benefits from music.

Quality of medical services is evaluated by benchmarks of benefit that are also economical and efficient. NICU-MT is underutilized and improves both medical and developmental outcomes for infants while reducing medical costs. For these reasons, it is an important new benchmark of quality NICU care. It behooves the profession to describe and promulgate specialized NICU-MT treatment techniques. Because of the extreme fragility and unique needs of premature infants still undergoing fetal development, it is also timely that the music therapy profession begins to develop specialized training for clinical treatment in this area.

This article offers a perspective on NICU-MT by integrating music research with developmental theory, medical treatment, and music therapy clinical practice. It also provides suggestions for development of the specialization of NICU-MT.

Keywords: perspectives, NICU, premature, music, standards, evidence-based

Overview
When music research began in the NICU over 25 years ago, the concept of a music therapist serving on the treatment team for premature infants receiving intensive care was novel. Professionals were protective of their very fragile patients. Initially, there was resistance to music being added to the medically controlled environment, since all sound was perceived as noise, music was considered nonessential during intensive care treatment and possibly dangerous to the developing infant, and research from a field not traditionally included in medical treatment was viewed with suspicion. Today NICU-MT is well known nationally and evolving internationally. In 2004, a survey of NICU nurses in the U.S. demonstrated that 68% wanted music included in NICU treatment. Of the top 25 U.S. children’s hospitals, over 50% now provide NICU music therapy. Such practice is congruent with individualized developmental and family-centered care. Music therapists outside the U.S. sometimes use NICU-MT evidence-based methodologies and interventions such as infant-directed singing.

Over 50 research studies in refereed medical and music therapy journals provide multiple types of evidence-based NICU-MT methodology and document multiple benefits for premature infants. NICU medical staff and professionals are advocating the importance of auditory input, including music, for premature infant development. Animal studies demonstrate neurologic growth from fetal music listening that has relevance for maturation of premature infants, as do research results with newborns showing unique development related to music listening. NICU early intervention research shows improved outcomes, as do NICU-MT clinical applications of evidence-based methods. Many allied health professionals and NICU parent groups are embracing the benefits.

For the purposes of this paper, NICU-MT is specific to the treatment of premature infants while in the NICU and does not include music therapy for term newborns with medical problems or premature infants after discharge. Though NICU-MT has a strong evidence base, it is not yet a standard of medical care in the U.S. A recent addition to NICU care, Child Life Services, is already included in pediatric standards of care. If music therapy achieved similar status with a staffing ratio of 1 to 20, 856 NICU music therapists would be needed to serve 17,109 NICU beds in the U.S. alone. Striving for this level of service provision is a monumental task.

Currently, a formalized NICU-MT training program exists in the U.S. that awards a special certificate upon completion and requires the applicant to be a Board Certified Music Therapist. Hundreds of U.S. and international music therapists have been trained. It is timely that music therapy professionals come together to promulgate NICU-MT specialization information for training, clinical practice, and research and seek inclusion in NICU medical standards of care.

Need for Conceptual Continuity
Music therapy is diverse in approach and use across many agencies and types of client services. Other professionals may perceive such diversity as lack of focus. In the NICU, music therapists address infant medical, developmental, and learning issues, parent-infant attachment, parent stress, and sometimes bereavement or counseling issues. The more specific music therapists are in articulating benefits and outcomes, the greater the medical acceptance of music therapy as integral to NICU care.

The premature infant’s neurologic system is incomplete and very different from that of a term infant. Environmental stimuli such as music therapy can cause harm if not coordinated with fetal developmental milestones by gestational week. For very premature infants, the medical model of care is the gold standard, with developmental or psychosocial issues addressed later. Medical emphases change as the infant achieves viability and completes treatment for complications of organ immaturity. These intense needs raise concerns for infant welfare, liability, and professional credibility.

The perspective suggested here proposes that NICU music therapists match theories of music therapy use to developmental maturation measured in gestational weeks. Benefits can be articulated within the medical model using accepted outcome measures and without conflict with medical guidelines. Problems occur when music is used in the NICU without music therapy expertise, such as by volunteers or well-meaning caregivers unaware of overstimulation risks.

Music therapy research and clinical descriptions must include medical or developmental measures of benefit, such as physiologic indicators or developmental milestones. Music used should be fully described, including content, complexity, duration, and decibel level.

Research shows music is effective whether live or recorded. Though live music is preferred, limited numbers of music therapists necessitate broader methodologies. Parents and caregivers are often stressed and need support, further expanding the role of music therapy.

NICU Clinical Treatment Integrated With Developmental Care
Life-sustaining medical treatment is the top priority for severely premature infants. Developmental care is organized into three stages: survival/pacification, cautious stimulation, and transition to interactive stimulation. NICU music therapy services are integrated into these stages.

Survival/Pacification Stage (23–30 gestational weeks)
During this stage, no touching, interacting, or disturbance of sleep is recommended. Uninterrupted music listening with minimal alerting stimuli is appropriate. Research shows quiet, consistent music soothes infants, masks aversive stimuli, and enhances medical indicators such as oxygen saturation.

Cautious Stimulation Phase (30–34 gestational weeks)
Parents may provide passive touch. Kangaroo care is highly beneficial when permitted. Music listening during passive touch is beneficial. Interaction is contraindicated until the infant tolerates minimal stimulation.

Interactive/Discharge Phase (34–40 gestational weeks)
Key milestones include establishment of sleep-wake cycles, coordinated feeding, social interaction, tolerance of multimodal stimulation, and orientation to auditory and visual stimuli. Music supports these milestones and facilitates discharge readiness.

Family Issues
Attachment, caregiver stress, empowerment, education, and preparation for discharge are critical. Music therapy supports attachment, reduces stress, and facilitates early intervention training for families.

Medical, Developmental, and Social Benefits of NICU-MT
Evidence-based NICU music therapy has been reimbursed by insurance and shown to generate economic as well as clinical benefits. Clinical outcomes replicate research findings, supporting NICU-MT as standard care.

Specific problems addressed include neurologic hypersensitivity, failure to thrive, apnea, nonnutritive sucking, attachment disruption, cause-effect learning deficits, and delayed oral feeding. Music therapy interventions are matched to each problem and yield improved medical stability, development, and earlier discharge.

Need for NICU Music Therapy Specialization
Premature infants are among the most medically fragile populations served by music therapy. Specialized training and guidelines are necessary to ensure safety and effectiveness. Much required knowledge is not typically taught in music therapy preparation programs. Improper use of music can cause long-term harm.

Suggested Guidelines for NICU Music Therapy Specialization
NICU music therapists should complete specialized training, adhere to medical and developmental guidelines, comply with insurance documentation requirements, conduct research meeting medical standards, provide services seven days a week, maintain appropriate staffing ratios, and be recognized as the experts responsible for music use in the NICU.

The integration of research, developmental theory, and medical guidelines can stimulate international collaboration and dissemination of best practices. Premature infants worldwide will benefit if music therapy becomes an essential component of NICU care.
