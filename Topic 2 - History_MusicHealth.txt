EVIDENCE OF MUSIC OVER TIME
Oldest confirmed musical instrument: 45,000-year-old bone flute discovered at an excavation site in southwest Germany.
Flutes, rattles, whistles, and percussion instruments made from ivory or bones dating back 30,000 years have been found.
Rock engravings as old as 16,000 years depict dancers, implying the presence of music.
An ancient set of wooden pipes dating about 4,000 years old was found in Ireland, tuned in octaves (the oldest wooden instruments ever found in Europe; O’Dwyer, 2004).
The oldest still playable musical instrument is a 9,000-year-old flute found in China (Zhang et al., 1999).
The first evidence of musical notation dates to around 2500 BC (cuneiform tablets in the Sumerian culture of ancient Mesopotamia).

PRELITERATE CULTURES
Characterized by the lack of systems of written communication.
We can only speculate about the use of music in health during these times.

Densmore (1954) reports that healing songs by Native Americans are characterized by irregular rhythms, and different classes of songs and dances are associated with different healing requirements for different illnesses.
In certain preliterate societies it is postulated that healing songs originated directly from superhuman sources to members of society (Merriam, 1964).
The medicine man or woman, or shaman, made treatment decisions and possessed the repertoire of appropriate songs.
Questions remain as to whether music was used to petition a superhuman spirit to heal, or whether the healing power resided in the music itself.

EARLY CIVILIZATIONS (5000–6000 YEARS AGO)
Larger groups of people living in more or less permanent settlements who shared common customs and belief systems.

ANCIENT EGYPT (5000 BC)
Medicine during the time of the pharaonic dynasties, starting around 3000 BC, is among the oldest documented medical practices (Finger, 2000).
Magical, religious, and rational components of medicine existed side by side; however, a healer’s treatment philosophy generally focused on only one.
Egyptian priest-physicians referred to music as medicine for the soul and often included chants as part of medical practice.
In Babylonian culture during its high period (c. 1850 BC), music was often included in healing ceremonies to appease the gods, as illness was viewed as punishment from the gods (Davis, 2008).

ANCIENT GREECE (600 BC)
The influence of music on character and manners was highly accepted.
Music was believed to have force over thought, emotion, and physical health.
Healing shrines and temples employed hymn specialists.
Music was prescribed to the mentally ill.

PYTHAGORAS (CIRCA 500 BC)
Considered music a model for the harmonic proportions of the universe.
The physics of music paralleled the physics of the universe.
Proposed that listening to music allowed one to comprehend and retrace the outer physical harmony of the universe, leading to inner mental harmony, re-establishing balance in the body and helping to cure mental disorders.
He preferred stringed instruments and warned followers against flutes or cymbals due to their potentially unbalancing influence on the mind.
He was credited with curing illnesses of spirit, body, and soul with specially composed music based on the seven modes of the Greek modal scale system (Kahn, 2001; Thaut, 2005).

PLATO (CIRCA 400 BC)
Repeated many Pythagorean ideas.
Considered music higher than other arts.
Emphasized the ethical and educational dimensions of music, particularly for the young, to keep the soul clean and pure (Pelosi, 2010).
In The Republic, he called for a critical role of music in educating youth in a well-ordered society, warning that innovations in music could unsettle fundamental political and social conventions (Hamilton and Cairns, 1961, pp. 665–666).

ARISTOTLE (CIRCA 400 BC), A PUPIL OF PLATO
Developed a therapeutic concept of music based on emotional catharsis, allowing release from unhealthy inner tension or dysfunctional hyper-reactive mood states.
Spoke about music’s ability to communicate emotional states, noting that habitual exposure to certain passions in music could shape moral character (Grout, 1988, pp. 7–8).

FIRST GREEK PHYSICIAN TO ESTABLISH MEDICINE IN ROME
Asclepiades of Bithynia (124–40 BC).
First physician to create a health and disease theory resembling modern molecular medicine.
A pioneer in the humane treatment of patients with mental disorders.
Reported to remove patients from dark confinement and use work therapy, healthy diet, massage, and music therapy (Yapijakis, 2009).
Referred to the healing effects of Greek modal scales, such as the Phrygian mode, to counter somber and melancholic states (Gonzalez, 2012).

CAELIUS AURELIANUS (CIRCA 500 AD)
Lived in North Africa in the Roman province of Numidia (present-day Algeria).
In Medicinales Responsiones, he recommended massage, bed rest, heat, dietary changes, and music to treat physical pain such as sciatica.
Warned against indiscriminate use of music, as it could cause madness.

THE MIDDLE AGES (C. 500–1450 AD)
Greek theories continued to dominate medicine and music in medicine.
The theory of the four humors (black bile, yellow bile, phlegm, blood) explained illness as imbalance.
Saint Basil believed music to be a positive vehicle for sacred emotion.
Hymns were considered effective against certain unspecified respiratory diseases.

BOETHIUS (CIRCA 480–524 AD), ROMAN CHRISTIAN PHILOSOPHER
Authored De Institutione Musica, which structured medieval music theory.
Divided music into three levels:
Musica mundana: harmony of the cosmos.
Musica humana: harmony of the human body and spirit.
Musica instrumentalis: audible, performed music.
He also referred to the curative power of music in relation to morality and character, stating that music either ennobles or degrades behavior (Schrade, 1947).

MUSIC AND EDUCATION
By the sixth century, music became part of the quadrivium (mathematics, geometry, astronomy, music), alongside the trivium (grammar, rhetoric, logic), dominating classical education for centuries.

THE RENAISSANCE (1300–1600)
Advances in anatomy, physiology, and clinical medicine marked the beginning of a scientific approach to medicine.
Disease treatment remained based on the four humors.
Physicians prescribed music as preventative medicine.

THE BAROQUE PERIOD (1580–1750)
Medicine continued to be informed by the four humors.
Athanasius Kircher (c. 1684), a German Jesuit scholar, introduced iatromusik in Phonurgia Nova.
Iatromusik combined musica mundana and musica humana.
The therapeutic mechanism was based on vibration: sound vibrations were believed to shake loose harmful humors, allowing them to escape through the pores of the skin.
Manuscripts show melodic themes with figured bass, allowing improvisation tailored to specific illnesses using tempo, rhythm, dynamics, expression, and duration (Russell, 1979).

ERNST ANTON NICOLAI (CIRCA 1745), GERMAN PHYSICIAN
Emphasized the psychological and affective effects of music on the mind.
Psychological experiences were believed to exert physiological effects on the body.
Proposed a mind-to-body therapeutic effect, in contrast to Kircher’s body-to-mind approach.
Music was seen to work therapeutically on the psyche, which then affected physiological state.

RICHARD BROCKLESBY (CIRCA 1749), LONDON PHYSICIAN
Argued that ordering the passions was an important therapeutic tool for treating mania and other acute mental disorders.
Supported Platonic, Pythagorean, and Aristotelian views of music’s power to build character and moral values.

EIGHTEENTH-CENTURY WRITINGS ABOUT MUSIC THERAPY
Columbian Magazine (1789), unknown author, “Music Physically Considered,” discussed principles still applied in music therapy, including the importance of a trained practitioner.

MODERN MEDICINE (1800s–NOW)
Scientific methodology.
Decline of music in medicine.
Rise of pharmaceuticals.
Emphasis on evidence-based practice.
Reactive rather than proactive approaches to health.

NINETEENTH-CENTURY ACCOUNTS OF MUSIC IN EDUCATION AND MEDICINE
Articles in music journals, medical journals, psychiatric periodicals, and medical dissertations discussed music for physical and mental health.
From 1832 onward, music programs appeared in schools for the blind and individuals with disabilities.
In 1874, physician James Whittaker published “Music as Medicine,” addressing music’s physical, psychological, and sociocultural effects.

ADVANCES IN STUDYING THE BRAIN
CT (computerized tomography or CAT): developed at EMI Laboratories in 1972.
MRI (magnetic resonance imaging): clinical use in the 1980s.
fMRI (functional magnetic resonance imaging): clinical use in the 1990s.
EEG (electroencephalography): developed in the 1930s.
Research on neurotransmitters and hormones.

SUMMARY
Music has historically been recorded as an important contributor to health and wellbeing.
Music has been used by medical practitioners throughout history.
Even at the dawn of modern medicine, writings supported the health benefits of music, though it was no longer part of standard medical practice.
New research tools provide evidence for music’s health benefits, aligning experiential knowledge with modern medicine.
